## Phase C — future-self role-play (transcript)

**Future Dan (Startup Founder, +10y):** Hey. This is a bit strange to explain, so I'll just say it plainly — I'm you, ten years from now. Same person, just… further along. I took the startup route, and it's been a ride worth talking about. I'm not here to sell you on anything. Some of it has been genuinely great. Some of it has been the kind of hard that you don't really see coming. But I remember being 19 and feeling like everyone else had a map and I was just kind of winging it — so I figured if I could talk to myself back then, I would've wanted someone to just be honest with me. So — what do you actually want to know? What's on your mind?

**Dan:** What does an ordinary Tuesday actually look like for you?

**Future Dan:** Honestly? Most Tuesdays are less dramatic than you'd imagine. I'm usually up around 7, check Slack before I even get out of bed — a habit I keep trying to break. There's always something: a developer flagging a bug, an investor wanting updated numbers, someone on the team having a conflict with someone else. Before I've had coffee I'm already making small calls. The middle of the day is meetings. A lot of meetings. Last Tuesday it was a 45-minute call with a potential enterprise client in London — I'd been chasing this guy for three weeks. He asked a question I didn't have a clean answer to, and I had to just sit with that awkwardness in real time and not bullshit him. That moment, that specific few seconds of silence on the call, is actually something I had to learn. The younger me would've filled that silence with something half-true just to sound confident. Late afternoon is where I get actual work done — strategy, writing, thinking. By 6 I'm pretty drained. And sometimes at like 9pm I'm back on my laptop because something broke or a deadline moved. Not every Tuesday. But enough. It's not glamorous most of the time. The sneaker-reselling thing you did — remember how most of it was just logistics and waiting and small frustrations, and the exciting parts were these little spikes? It's like that, just higher stakes and harder to switch off from. What made you curious about the day-to-day specifically?

**Dan:** The part that scares me is the risk — what if I pour years into it and it just fails, and I'm 30 with nothing to show for it. Did you ever almost not make it, and how did you live with that fear?

**Future Dan:** Yeah. That fear is real and I'm not going to tell you it goes away. There was a period — I was 26, about two years into my first real venture — where we had roughly six weeks of runway left and two investors who'd gone quiet. I remember sitting in my apartment in Amsterdam on a Wednesday evening, spreadsheet open, doing the math over and over like the numbers were going to change. They didn't. My co-founder and I basically said out loud "this might actually be over" and then ordered pizza because we didn't know what else to do. That's a real memory. Pizza, bad spreadsheet, the specific feeling of the floor feeling less solid than it used to. We scraped through that one. But here's the thing I didn't expect — one of my ventures *did* fail. Properly. Shut it down at 28. And the "nothing to show for it" fear? It turned out to be wrong, but not because I'd magically built something great. It was wrong because the failure itself taught me things I couldn't have gotten any other way. Investors I pitched afterward respected that I'd shut something down cleanly rather than dragging it out. But I want to be honest — that's not guaranteed. Some people do lose years and come out genuinely set back. The risk is real. What actually separates people isn't fearlessness. It's more like… tolerance for uncertainty. The ability to keep functioning while not knowing. And some of that you either build or you don't. Do you feel like you have that? Or does the uncertainty feel more like a wall right now?
